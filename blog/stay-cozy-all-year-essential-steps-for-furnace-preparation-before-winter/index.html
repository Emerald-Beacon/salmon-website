Stay Cozy All Year: Essential Steps for Furnace Preparation Before Winter

When temperatures dip across Northern Utah, there’s nothing better than stepping into a warm, comfortable home. But that comfort starts long before the first snowflake falls...

Salmon HVAC
Published July 21, 2025  •  Updated July 21, 2025  •  5 min read

When temperatures dip across Northern Utah, there’s nothing better than stepping into a warm, comfortable home. But that comfort starts long before the first snowflake falls. Preparing your furnace before winter ensures efficient performance, lower energy costs, and uninterrupted warmth during the coldest months.

Whether you’re in Salt Lake, Layton, or Centerville, these essential steps will help your heating system work smarter, not harder.

Why Furnace Maintenance Is a Must in Utah

Winter in Utah can bring frigid lows, dry air, and fluctuating conditions that put extra strain on HVAC systems. Routine maintenance ensures your furnace keeps up without breaking down.

Regular inspections and cleanings can increase energy efficiency by up to 15 percent and extend the lifespan of your furnace. Neglect, on the other hand, can lead to sudden breakdowns, safety hazards, and high utility bills.

Don’t wait until your furnace fails on a freezing night. Preventive care is the key to peace of mind.

How to Prepare Your Furnace for Winter

Let’s break down the core steps to ready your furnace for a Utah winter:

Clean or Replace Your Filters

Your furnace filter does more than trap dust. It protects the entire system from buildup that can reduce airflow and force your furnace to work overtime. In areas like Bountiful or Farmington, where winter air is especially dry and dusty, this becomes even more important.

Check your filter monthly and replace or clean it every 1 to 3 months. A clean filter improves indoor air quality and system performance.

Inspect and Clean Internal Components

Before you fire up your system for the season, check key components:

  • Burners should be free of soot or buildup.
  • The blower motor must be clean and lubricated.
  • Exhaust flues should be clear of blockages.
  • Thermostat connections must be tight and calibrated.

Even a small amount of dust or debris can reduce performance and lead to mechanical issues. If this sounds overwhelming, a qualified HVAC technician can handle it as part of a seasonal tune-up.

Seal Up Duct Leaks

If warm air escapes before reaching your rooms, your furnace works harder and your bills go up. Duct leaks are common in older homes throughout Utah and can account for up to 30 percent of heat loss.

Inspect ductwork in basements and attics for gaps or damage. Sealing leaks with mastic tape or having a professional seal and insulate ducts can significantly increase efficiency.

Professional Tune-Ups: A Smart Winter Prep Step

Calling a trusted HVAC professional like Salmon HVAC before the first deep freeze helps you avoid mid-winter emergencies. A certified technician will:

  • Check and clean heat exchangers and burners
  • Test carbon monoxide levels
  • Ensure proper airflow and combustion
  • Tighten connections and lubricate moving parts

This service often uncovers small issues before they grow into costly breakdowns.

Upgrade to a Smart Thermostat for Winter Efficiency

Smart thermostats are one of the easiest ways to optimize your home’s heating. They let you:

  • Schedule temperature changes based on your routine
  • Control heating remotely with your phone
  • Automatically reduce heat while you’re away
  • Review energy usage reports to find cost-saving opportunities

Many Utah homeowners use smart thermostats to maintain comfort while minimizing energy use. In winter, you can set temperatures lower at night or while you’re away, then warm the home before you return all without lifting a finger.

Look Beyond the Furnace: Whole-Home Winter Comfort

A warm furnace is only part of the story. Whole-home strategies can improve heat retention and overall comfort.

Improve Insulation

Insufficient insulation in attics and crawl spaces lets valuable heat escape. Upgrading insulation can reduce furnace runtime and save on energy bills. Aim for R-49 in attics and R-19 or higher in walls for Northern Utah.

Seal Drafts Around Windows and Doors

Cold air sneaks in through tiny cracks. Use weather stripping, caulk, and door sweeps to create a tight barrier. Check around baseboards, window frames, and outlets too.

Use Zone Heating Where Possible

Zoning systems allow you to heat only the rooms you use. For larger homes or multi-level layouts in places like Kaysville or West Jordan, zone control helps prevent hot and cold spots while using less energy.

Signs Your Furnace Needs Immediate Attention

Be alert for warning signs that something’s wrong:

  • Unusual noises like banging or grinding
  • Uneven heating across rooms
  • Strange odors or increased dust
  • Short cycling or frequent system restarts
  • Unexplained spikes in energy bills

If any of these issues appear, schedule a service call right away. Delaying repairs can cause further damage or even safety hazards.

FAQs: Furnace Prep in Utah

How early should I prepare my furnace before winter?

Ideally, schedule a tune-up in early fall before you need to use your furnace daily. This ensures your system is safe, efficient, and ready when temperatures drop.

What temperature should I set on my thermostat in winter?

For optimal comfort and energy savings, set your thermostat to around 68°F while home, and reduce it to 60–62°F while sleeping or away.

How do I know if my furnace is energy-efficient?

Look for an AFUE rating of 90% or higher. Older systems may be less efficient and could benefit from an upgrade. Salmon HVAC can assess your unit and offer energy-saving solutions.

Can I inspect my furnace myself?

You can check filters and look for visible issues, but most inspections especially carbon monoxide testing and internal component checks are best left to professionals.

Is furnace maintenance really necessary every year?

Yes. Annual maintenance keeps your system running efficiently, catches safety concerns early, and extends the life of your furnace. It’s a small investment that pays off in comfort and savings.
