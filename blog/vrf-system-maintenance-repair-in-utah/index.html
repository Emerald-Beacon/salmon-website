What Is a VRF System — and Why Does Maintenance Matter?

Variable Refrigerant Flow (VRF) is an advanced HVAC technology that uses refrigerant as the primary medium for heating and cooling, rather than ducted air or chilled water. A single outdoor condensing unit connects to multiple indoor air-handling units, each of which can be independently controlled for temperature and airflow. This makes VRF systems exceptionally energy-efficient and flexible — especially for buildings with diverse heating and cooling needs across different zones.

Quick Fact

VRF systems can achieve efficiency ratings (COPs) of 3 to 6 or higher, meaning they deliver 3–6 units of heating or cooling energy for every unit of electricity consumed — making them among the most efficient commercial HVAC options available.

That efficiency, however, depends entirely on the system being properly maintained. VRF systems rely on precise refrigerant charge levels, clean heat exchangers, and well-calibrated controls. When any one of these drifts out of specification — often gradually, without obvious symptoms — energy consumption climbs, comfort drops, and the risk of a costly repair event increases significantly.

In Utah's climate, where a single building may need to heat aggressively in January and cool just as hard in July, your VRF system works hard year-round. Regular professional maintenance is the most cost-effective investment you can make in the long-term performance of the system.

Common VRF System Repairs We Handle in Utah

Even well-maintained VRF systems eventually need repairs. Here are the most common issues our technicians encounter on service calls across Salt Lake City and the surrounding area — and what resolving them typically involves.

1. Refrigerant Leaks

VRF systems use significantly more refrigerant piping than traditional split systems, which means there are more potential leak points. A slow refrigerant leak often presents first as reduced cooling capacity in one or more zones, followed by increased energy consumption as the system works harder to compensate. Our technicians use electronic leak detectors and nitrogen pressure testing to locate leaks accurately, perform a certified repair, and restore the system to proper charge.

2. Communication Faults Between Indoor and Outdoor Units

VRF systems rely on a continuous digital communication network between all components. A wiring fault, faulty PCB (printed circuit board), or interference on the communication bus can cause individual indoor units — or in some cases, the entire system — to stop responding correctly. We trace communication errors systematically using manufacturer diagnostic software and replace faulty components as needed.

3. Compressor Issues

The inverter-driven compressor is the heart of any VRF system. Compressor failures are typically preceded by warning signs: unusual noise, increased vibration, elevated discharge temperatures, or fault codes from the system's controller. Catching and addressing these early — rather than running the compressor until it fails completely — can mean the difference between a repair and a full compressor replacement.

4. Expansion Valve Problems

Electronic expansion valves (EEVs) modulate refrigerant flow to each indoor unit. A sticking or failed EEV typically causes one zone to lose capacity while the rest of the system operates normally. Our technicians can diagnose and replace EEVs on most major VRF brands.

5. Condensate Drainage Issues

Indoor VRF units produce condensate that must drain properly. Blocked or poorly pitched condensate lines lead to water damage and mold risk. This is one of the most preventable issues — and one of the reasons we check every condensate drain on every maintenance visit.

⚠ Don't Ignore Fault Codes. VRF systems are highly communicative — when something is wrong, they tell you with error codes. If you're seeing fault codes on your VRF controller or building management system, don't dismiss them. Call Salmon HVAC and we'll interpret the codes and get to the root cause before a minor issue becomes a major one.

Recommended VRF Maintenance Schedule for Utah Properties

Utah's climate puts distinctive demands on HVAC systems — cold winters, hot summers, and dry air throughout. We recommend the following maintenance cadence for VRF systems in our service area:

  • Twice a year (spring and fall): Full system inspection and tune-up. Spring service prepares the system for cooling season; fall service ensures it's ready for heating mode. This is the foundation of a solid VRF maintenance program.
  • Monthly (owner/operator): Visually inspect indoor unit filters and clean or replace per manufacturer guidance. Check condensate drain pans for standing water. Note any new fault codes or unusual sounds.
  • Annually: Refrigerant system leak check with electronic detection equipment. Cleaning of outdoor coils. Comprehensive controls and communication test. Review of system performance data if the system is connected to a building management system.
  • Every 3–5 years: Comprehensive refrigerant circuit integrity test. Assessment of compressor health. Evaluation of whether software/firmware updates are available from the manufacturer.

Frequently Asked Questions About VRF System Service

How do I know if my VRF system needs immediate repair vs. routine maintenance?

Signs that warrant a prompt service call include: active fault codes on the controller, one or more zones that have stopped heating or cooling entirely, unusual noises from the outdoor unit, ice formation on refrigerant lines, or visible water leaking from indoor units. For anything that doesn't fall into those categories, schedule a routine maintenance visit and we'll evaluate the system's overall health.

Can any HVAC company service a VRF system?

Technically, yes — but not every company should. VRF systems have sophisticated controls, proprietary communication protocols, and refrigerant circuits that behave differently from conventional systems. Servicing them correctly requires brand-specific training and tools. Inexperienced service on a VRF system can void manufacturer warranties and create new problems. Always ask whether your HVAC provider has specific VRF experience before booking.

How long do VRF systems last?

With proper maintenance, VRF systems typically last 15–20 years. Compressors are generally the first major component to reach end of life. Regular maintenance significantly extends that lifespan by catching small issues before they cause wear and tear on major components.

Do you service all VRF brands?

We service the major brands commonly found throughout Utah, including Mitsubishi Electric, Daikin, LG, and others. Contact us with your system's make and model and we'll confirm our capability before your appointment.
